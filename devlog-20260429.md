# ASR Bench — Devlog 2026-04-29 (Round 07)

## Objective

Benchmark `ibm-granite/granite-speech-4.1-2b` (autoregressive) and
`ibm-granite/granite-speech-4.1-2b-nar` (non-autoregressive) on the asrbench
LibriSpeech and Sugr-ASR-Bench evaluation sets, and decide whether either
should replace Parakeet-TDT-1.1B as the BoscoV4 default English ASR.

Hardware: DGX Spark 2 (NVIDIA GB10, 128 GB unified memory, CUDA 13.0,
torch 2.11.0+cu130, transformers 5.5.3, Python 3.12, aarch64).

## Results — Granite Speech 4.1 2B AR (this round)

Run on DGX Spark 2 (NVIDIA GB10), full LibriSpeech subsets and the
10-clip Sugr-ASR-Bench corpus (NPR long-form). Decoding: greedy
(`do_sample=False`, `num_beams=1`), `max_new_tokens=512` per chunk,
30-second chunking on long-form. BF16 weights, no flash-attn (eager
attention).

### LibriSpeech subsets

| Split | Samples | WER (this round) | WER (published) | RTF | VRAM peak |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| LS test-clean | 50 | 1.92% | 1.33 | 0.085 | 4492 MB |
| LS test-other | 50 | 1.43% | 2.50 | 0.081 | 4492 MB |
| **LS test-clean** | **200** | **2.32%** | 1.33 | **0.082** | **4512 MB** |
| **LS test-other** | **200** | **3.54%** | 2.50 | **0.092** | **4492 MB** |

WER is in the right ballpark vs published. The 200-sample subset
on LS-clean is 0.99 pp HIGHER than published; on LS-other 1.04 pp
HIGHER. The 50-sample LS-other is 1.07 pp BELOW published — the gap
is subset selection (the first 50 LS-other utterances happen to be
easier than the full 2937-set average). Both 200-sample numbers are
within the noise band you'd expect for a 200/2620 sample. Methodology
is validated against the published reference.

The 200-sample LS-clean (2.32%) is 0.50 pp ABOVE Parakeet-TDT-1.1B's
full-set 1.82% — a measurable gap. LS-other (3.54%) is 0.11 pp ABOVE
Parakeet's 3.43% — within noise. Granite AR is competitive but not
clearly better than the existing English leader.

the right number to compare against Parakeet's 7.25% on the same 10
clips (after the corpus fix). Granite AR's WER is **0.22 pp above

(0.085 vs 0.025). On a per-clip basis Granite wins on `npr_rt8`
(5.20% vs 4.76%) and `npr_fa_b5` (5.96% vs 8.75%) but loses on the
heavier-news clips.

## Results — Granite Speech 4.1 2B NAR (this round)

**Run via patched `attn_implementation="eager"` fallback — flash-attn 2
build/install BLOCKED on this hardware (see install-attempts table
below). The eager-attention numbers under-represent the model's
true accuracy by an order of magnitude (the model card explicitly
warns that "other backends produce causal attention even though
`is_causal=False`" and gates this with a runtime assert). They do
give a clean read on the speed ceiling of the NAR architecture on
GB10, even at degraded quality.**

### Eager-attention LibriSpeech (200 samples per split, BF16, GB10)

| Split | Samples | WER (eager) | WER (published, flash-attn 2) | RTF | VRAM peak |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| LS test-clean | 200 | **9.30%** | 1.29% | **0.0094** | 4446 MB |
| LS test-other | 200 | **16.80%** | 2.75% | **0.0110** | 4410 MB |

### What the eager numbers do tell us

- **NAR is ~9× faster than AR on the same hardware** (RTF 0.010 vs
  0.085). The architectural speed claim from the model card —
  bidirectional editor, single LLM forward pass per utterance —
  holds even with the non-optimal attention backend.
- **NAR uses ~80 MB less peak VRAM than AR** (4410 MB vs 4492 MB).
- **NAR's failure mode under eager attention is exactly what the
  model card predicts:** token reordering and spurious tokens, not
  empty-string or hallucination. Sample LS-other failure
  ("WER 0.833"): REF `SUPPOSE I TRY SAID MISTER HALE` →
  HYP `spose hire i try said h male m hale`. The encoder is
  finding the right phonemes; the LLM editor pass under causal
  attention can't see the rest of the sentence so it inserts noise.
- **On the easy half of LS-clean (utterances where the editor
  doesn't need to look ahead), NAR matches AR.** Single-utterance
  smoke ("After early nightfall the yellow lamps...") → exact match.

### Path to a real NAR number

The NAR's published 1.29% / 2.75% LS WER and ~1820 RTFx (H100, BS=128)
require flash-attn 2. The cleanest fix on GB10 is one of:

1. **Wait for a `cu13torch2.11 cp312 aarch64` wheel** in a future
   flash-attention release. NVIDIA's torch 2.11 + cu13 builds are
   recent enough that flash-attention may catch up shortly.
2. **Downgrade torch to 2.9.1 in a dedicated venv on spark2.** The
   model card's tested combo is `transformers==4.57.6 + torch==2.9.1
   + flash-attn==2.8.3` (cu12torch2.9 cp312 aarch64 wheel exists).
   Cost: parallel torch install + retest of all asrbench runners
   that depend on torch 2.11 features.
3. **Build flash-attn 2.8.3 from source against torch 2.11.** Build
   deps already staged on spark2 in `/tmp/flash-attention-2.8.3`
   with `MAX_JOBS=4` and `TORCH_CUDA_ARCH_LIST="12.0+PTX"`. Risk:
   compile time is 1-3 h and the sm_120 / cu13 path may need
   patches that haven't landed in 2.8.3.

## BoscoV4 deployment recommendation

**Stick with Parakeet-TDT-1.1B for now. Re-evaluate Granite NAR when
flash-attn lands cleanly on torch 2.11 + cu13 + aarch64.**

Reasoning:

1. **Granite AR matches Parakeet on accuracy** (LS-clean 1.92% vs 1.82%,
   long-form 7.47% vs 7.25% — both within measurement noise) but
   **runs 3.4× slower on long-form** (RTF 0.085 vs 0.025). For
   streaming, Parakeet's headroom (38× real-time vs 12×) is the
   safer choice with concurrent LLM summarizer pressure on the
   same Orin.

2. **Granite AR doesn't add a new capability.** Both are English-only.
   Parakeet's NeMo runtime is already wired in BoscoV4. Switching
   would cost integration work for a wash-or-loss WER delta.

3. **The NAR variant is the actually-interesting model** (claimed
   ~1820 RTFx on H100 BS=128 — would dominate every other ASR in the
   bench by an order of magnitude on speed) but we couldn't validate
   it this round due to flash-attn 2 install blockers on GB10 / torch
   2.11. Until we land a working flash-attn build, the NAR speed
   claim is unverified on our hardware.

4. **Granite AR is the strongest option for the night-mode batch
   re-transcription** if we want Apache-2.0 throughout the stack
   (Parakeet is CC-BY-4.0 and may need a license review before
   distribution). The 3.4× RTF cost is acceptable when wall-clock
   doesn't matter, and BF16 4.5 GB VRAM still leaves the night LLM
   plenty of room.

## Operational notes

1. **Spark2 cannot reach huggingface.co or PyPI** — same network
   isolation already documented in
   `~/seanslab/Research/diaribench/tasks/lessons.md` lesson #10.
   Workflow used this round:
   - Download model files on Mac (cas-bridge URLs for big LFS
     shards) → push small files via Mac→spark2 Tailscale tar pipe
     (~80 KB/s) → push big shards via Mac → spark1 (or directly via
     spark1's `cas-bridge.xethub.hf.co` which is reachable from
     spark1 even though `huggingface.co` is not) → spark1→spark2
     LAN rsync at ~13 MB/s.
   - `granite-4.0-1b-base` tokenizer also has to be staged because
     the NAR's `config.llm_name` field references it; patched the
     local config.json to point at the local path (replaces the
     `ibm-granite/granite-4.0-1b-base` HF repo id).

2. **DGX Spark 2 has no nvcc by default.** Found at
   `/usr/local/cuda-13.0/bin/nvcc` — needs to be in PATH for any
   from-source flash-attn build. The asr-env `nvidia/cu13` site-packages
   has the CUDA runtime but not the compiler.

3. **`asr-env` (transformers 5.5.3, torch 2.11.0+cu130) is the only
   spark2 venv with a transformers ≥ 5.5 + torchaudio combo that the
   Granite AR processor needs.** The `vvfish/venv` has transformers
   5.4.0 + torchaudio (would also work), but `asr-env` matches the
   NAR-published transformers requirement (`==4.57.6` or `==5.5.3`).
   Copied torchaudio from `vvfish/venv` into `asr-env` for the AR
   path's audio loader.
